## Cybersecurity
- Cybersecurity is the practice of protecting networks, devices, applications, and digital assets from unauthorized access, cyberattacks, and data breaches. It integrates people, policies, and technologies to ensure the confidentiality, integrity, and availability of information against threats like ransomware, phishing, and malware. [1, 2]  
- Modern cybersecurity is divided into several core domains and utilizes key defense principles to secure both individual and enterprise environments.

## Core Domains 
- `Network Security`: Protects internal networks and architecture by utilizing tools like firewalls and continuous traffic monitoring.
- `Cloud Security`: Secures cloud infrastructure and data at rest, in motion, and in use.
- `Endpoint Security`: Protects individual devices (e.g., computers, smartphones) from threats targeting system entry points.
- `Application Security`: Ensures safe software functionality by mitigating code vulnerabilities during development and deployment.

## Key Cybersecurity Principles & Best Practices 
- `Zero Trust`: A security architecture where no user, device, or network transaction is inherently trusted, requiring continuous verification. 
- `Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)`: Requires multiple forms of verification to access electronic information, drastically reducing unauthorized account access. 
- `Cyber Hygiene`: Basic routine practices such as maintaining strong passwords, keeping operating systems updated, and recognizing suspicious emails or links. 
- `Incident Response & Backups`: Enforcing tested, offline data backups and having a clear plan to recover quickly if an attack succeeds.

## The AI Shift 
- Artificial intelligence is heavily reshaping the threat landscape. While attackers are leveraging AI to scale deepfakes, automated exploits, and phishing campaigns, defenders are utilizing AI and machine learning for rapid threat detection and automated incident response. 
- To explore official guidelines and services, review the Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency (CISA) for actionable cyber hygiene tips. 

## Networking in cybersecurity 
- It is the foundation for defending digital systems. It involves understanding how data travels across devices (the OSI model, IP addresses, ports) so security professionals can monitor traffic, detect anomalies, block malicious payloads, and protect network infrastructure from cyber threats.
- A deep grasp of networking is vital for investigating breaches, designing architectures, and implementing defenses. Critical networking concepts for cybersecurity include: 

- The OSI and TCP/IP Models: Frameworks that describe how data moves from physical hardware to software applications. Understanding these layers allows professionals to pinpoint where an attack (like a packet flood or protocol misuse) occurs. 
- Protocols and Ports: Rules that govern data exchange (e.g., TCP, UDP, HTTP, DNS). Securing these involves monitoring for unauthorized access, encrypting traffic (using TLS/SSL), and closing or securing vulnerable ports. 
- Hardware and Appliances: Firewalls, routers, switches, and intrusion detection/prevention systems (IDS/IPS). These devices enforce security policies by controlling what traffic is allowed in or out of a network. 
- Network Segmentation: Dividing a larger network into smaller, isolated subnets to contain the spread of malware or unauthorized access if a breach occurs. 
- Traffic Analysis and Monitoring: Using tools like Wireshark or SIEMs to trace data packets, interpret logs, and identify suspicious activity (e.g., unauthorized data exfiltration or DDoS attacks).  

Experts agree that while the depth of knowledge required depends on the cybersecurity specialization, fundamental networking is essential for effective threat hunting, incident response, and building resilient systems. 
To explore core networking fundamentals and security principles, refer to educational guides by networking leaders like Cisco or IBM.
