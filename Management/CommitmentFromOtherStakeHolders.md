# Getting Commitment from Peer Managers and Involving Higher Management

Managing dependencies across teams requires effective collaboration and clear communication, especially when requirements have tight dependencies on peer managers. Here’s how to approach this and when to escalate to higher management:

---

## 1. Getting Commitment from Peer Managers

### Build Strong Relationships

- Establish trust and rapport with peer managers through regular communication.
- Understand their priorities, constraints, and challenges.

### Clearly Communicate Requirements and Impact

- Present the requirement clearly, emphasizing its importance and impact on overall delivery.
- Explain how their commitment affects project success and mutual goals.

### Collaborate on Planning

- Involve peer managers early in planning discussions.
- Co-create timelines and resource plans that consider their team’s capacity.

### Negotiate and Align Priorities

- Be open to negotiation and find win-win solutions.
- Align on shared objectives and dependencies to foster joint ownership.

### Formalize Agreements

- Document commitments in project plans, meeting notes, or collaboration tools.
- Use these documents as reference points to track progress.

### Maintain Continuous Engagement

- Provide regular updates and check-ins.
- Address concerns promptly and adjust plans collaboratively.

---

## 2. When to Involve Higher Management

### Appropriate Stages to Involve Higher Management

- **Early Escalation for Alignment:**  
  When cross-team dependencies impact strategic priorities or require resource reallocation beyond your control.

- **Conflict Resolution:**  
  If peer managers are unable or unwilling to commit despite efforts to collaborate.

- **Risk Mitigation:**  
  When delays or issues in dependent teams pose significant risks to critical delivery dates.

- **Resource Constraints:**  
  When additional resources or organizational support are needed to meet commitments.

### When Not to Involve Higher Management

- **Initial Coordination:**  
  Avoid escalating before attempting direct communication and negotiation with peer managers.

- **Minor Issues:**  
  For small blockers or routine coordination that can be resolved within teams.

- **Micromanagement:**  
  Avoid involving higher management in day-to-day task-level details.

---

## Summary

Getting commitment from peer managers requires building relationships, clear communication, collaborative planning, and continuous engagement. Higher management should be involved strategically—primarily for alignment, conflict resolution, risk mitigation, and resource needs—while routine coordination should be handled directly between teams.
